Two fundamental metrics of tumor metabolism are total glucose (Glc) consumption and the relative fraction of Glc
undergoing glycolysis (GLY) versus oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) 1 . Further, the Warburg effect, as part of
tumor metabolic reprogramming in which GLY is favored
over OXPHOS, is a hallmark of cancer and is particularly
pronounced in glioma and other brain tumors 2,3,4 . From a
therapeutic perspective, multiple studies indicate that this
Warburg phenotype is critical to the cancer process, thus

providing the basis for developing novel therapies in which 93
altering the fluxes through these metabolic pathways is the 94
goal 5,6,7,8,9,10,11,12 . We contend that clinical translation is 95
presently impeded not so much by a lack of agents, but by the 96
difficulty in measuring tumor metabolism in vivo 5,13 , namely 97
total glucose consumption and the relative fraction of glucose 98
99
undergoing GLY versus OXPHOS.

There are several MRI techniques that provide information on glucose metabolism. MRI has been used to measure cerebral blood flow (CBF) but is not yet validated for
cerebral metabolic rate of oxygen (CMRO2), a measure of
OXPHOS 23 . pH imaging using CEST provides an indirect
measure of lactate (Lac) and hence GLY 24 . 1 H MRS of lactate can assess lactate pool sizes, indirectly related to GLY,
however, contain no flux information and often suffer from
magnetic field inhomogeneities and overlapping lipids 25 . In
contrast, hyperpolarized 13 C (HP13C) MRSI is an emerging technology offering a dramatically enhanced signal-tonoise ratio (SNR) over conventional MRS methods 26,27,28 .
[1-13 C]pyruvate (Pyr), the first hyperpolarized substrate to
reach clinical trials, has generated significant interest due to
Pyr occupying a critical branch point in glucose metabolism
whereby it can be reduced to Lac (i.e. GLY), decarboxylated
to acetyl-CoA (generating bicarbonate (Bic) in the process)
as the first step towards OXPHOS, or transaminated to alanine 29 . Primary HP13C applications include cancer and heart
disease 27,28,29,30,31,32,33 . Whereas most HP13C cancer studies
have focused on Lac 27,28,29,34 , we hypothesized that the 13 C
Lac/Bic ratio is a surrogate marker for GLY/OXPHOS 5,35,36 .
The 2 H MRI of the metabolic products of deuterated glucose, known as deuterium metabolic imaging (DMI), is the
most recently proposed in vivo glucose metabolic imaging
method 37 . As discussed below, we believe DMI has the potential to be both the most accurate and clinically viable method
for imaging GLY and OXPHOS.

1.3

Deuterium Metabolic Imaging

The recent introduction of DMI offers a highly attractive alternative for brain tumor metabolic imaging 38 . de Feyter et al.
showed DMI can map the balance of glycolysis and mitochondrial metabolism in patients with glioma at 4T 60-75 min
following oral consumption of [6,6’-2 H2 ]Glc 37 . Analogous
to the 13 C Lac/Bic ratio, the ratio of 2 H-Lac/2 H-Glx (glutamate+glutamine) provides a direct measure of the Warburg

effect. Furthermore, DMI avoids the 1 H MRS need for water 54
and lipid suppression due to the low natural abundance of 55
2
H, and its low Larmor frequency minimizes sensitivity to 56
B0 inhomogeneities 39,40 . Although much slower than HP13C 57
(∼2 vs 30 min acquisition), DMI requires no FDA Investiga- 58
tional New Drug approval, no intravenous injection, no tight 59
inject and imaging time restrictions, no spin polarizer and 60
uses less expensive materials (∼$600/study for human [6,6’- 61
2
H2 ]-Glc vs ∼$2-3k/injection for HP13C). Furthermore, the 62
safety of deuterated glucose in humans is well established via 63
multiple studies of metabolism in both adults and infants 41,42 . 64
In addition to the seminal DMI study in patients with 65
glioma at 4T by de Feyter et. al., multiple other sites have 66
begun DMI studies in healthy human subjects at 3T 43 , 7T 44 67
as well as at 9.4T 45 . In preclinical animal models, Kreis, 68
et al, showed that dynamic DMI acquisitions at 9.4T could 69
be used to acquire quantitative and spatially resolved mea- 70
surements of glycolytic flux in tumors 15 , which can be used 71
to assess treatment response 46 . Batsios et. al. demonstrated 72
DMI using [U-2 H]-pyruvate for assessing early treatment 73
response in a preclinical model for reporting on TERT expres- 74
sion via assessing the production of lactate in mice with 75
76
patient-derived gliomas 47 .
If successfully demonstrated, DMI at 3T would acceler- 77
ate clinical translation due to the ubqiquity of clinical 3T 78
scanners. Feasibility studies of DMI at 3T in healthy controls 79
have been conducted by our group 48 , as well as by Kaggie 80
et. al. 43 . However, patient studies have yet to be conducted at 81
this clinical field strength, and optimization of hardware and 82
pulse sequences has yet to be explored. In this study, we per- 83
form experiments on healthy controls to optimize hardware 84
and pulse sequence parameters at 3T. We additionally con- 85
duct the first DMI patient studies at 3T to our knowledge, 86
exploring the ability to quantify local glucose metabolism in 87
correspondence to anatomical landmarks across three patients 88
89
with varying CNS lesions.

On our 3T PET/MRI Signa GE scanner, the gradient drivers 96
exhibit noise in the 19-20 MHz band that is not adequately 97
filtered using the standard gradient filters (consisting of com- 98

the gradient filter was modified so that half of the cores were 102
separated for differential filtering and the capacitors were 103
increased 8-fold (Figure 1 ). In addition to the modified gradi- 104
ent filter, 2 clip-on ferrite cores (Fair-Rite 0443164151) were 105
installed on each of the 12 gradient coil leads on both sides 106

2.2

RF Birdcage Coil

We constructed a 3T 2 H lowpass quadrature T/R birdcage
head coil built from high conductivity .25” copper tubing. The
coil has an inner diameter (id) of 266 mm (10.5"), ring spacing (z) of 266 mm (10.5"), and was tuned to 19.6 MHz and fed
with quarter wave feed lines. This yielded an unloaded/loaded
Q of 685/90 (Q ratio L/U 7.62). We then used a commercial
deuterium coil interface by Clinical MR Solutions designed
for 19.6 MHz quadrature TR operation. The interface contained a narrow band TR switch, low noise preamp, and a
quadrature splitter combiner.
To explore the impact of the birdcage coil design on SNR,
we additionally constructed a second coil with thicker copper
tubing (0.5"), 254 mm (10") id, and shorter z of 178 mm (7"),
yielding an unloaded/loaded Q of 703/104 (Q ratio L/U 6.75).
We hypothesize that the thicker tubing and shorter coil would
yield greater SNR due to the lower resistive losses. Both coils
were pre-tuned on the bench to 19.3 MHZ to account for
upward frequency shift in the magnet bore. The SNR between
the two coils were compared in a phantom study, described in
section 3.1.2

2.4

Data Processing

DMI datasets were processed using DMIWizard, an opensource Matlab-based toolbox 49 . Raw k-space data were zeropadded in both the spectral and spatial dimensions, phasecorrected, Fourier Transformed and spectrally apodised by
5 Hz. The subsequent spectra were quantified through leastsquares fitting to generate metabolic maps of deuterated water
(HDO), glucose (2 H-Glc), glutamine/glutamate (2 H-Glx) and

3

lactate (2 H-Lac). The ratio of 2 H-Lac/(2 H-Glx+2 H-Lac) was 54
used to quantify the fraction of glucose metabolism that 55
undergoes GLY (as opposed to OXPHOS), a possible measure 56
of the Warburg effect. The additional proton scans acquired 57
with a conventional multi-channel head coil were aligned to 58
the body-coil proton scan (acquired with the 2 H coil in place), 59
and therefore the DMI dataset, via 3D rigid registration using 60
61
the SimpleITK toolkit 50 .

2.5

Phantom Design

64
65

For the purposes of SNR comparison experiments, we con- 66
structed a phantom with relaxation properties comparable to 67
those reported in vivo in human brain tissue 37 . The phantom 68
was constructed with de-ionized water, 1.5% agar and 0.1% 69
d-methanol, and baked in under vacuum in an autoclave at 70
121°C for 10 minutes. SNR comparisons use the HDO peak 71
due to natural abundance deuterium in water, while the d- 72
methanol provides a second peak of comparable magnitude 73
for assessing spectral resolution.
74
The T1 of the phantom was measured using a non- 75
localized inversion recovery sequence with an adiabatic pulse, 76
5 second repetition time (TR), 900 𝜇𝑠 rectangular excitation 77
pulse, 256 samples, 1 kHz receive bandwidth, and 4 aver- 78
ages 51 . Twenty logarithmically spaced inversion times (TI) 79
between 25 and 2000 ms were measured. The water peak 80
amplitudes were then fit in Matlab R2022a (MATLAB, The 81
MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA) using the Matlab nonlinear 82
regression model fitting function. The agar concetration was 83
selected so the HDO relaxation parameters were close to those 84
of the relaxation parameters of HDO in brain at 4T 37 , yielding 85
a T1 relaxation time of 337 ms.

We conducted both phantom and normal volunteer studies to
quantify SNR efficiency across improvements to the gradient
filter, RF coil, and pulse sequence parameters. Raw data were
first processed as described in Section 2.4. SNR was computed by first estimating the signal in each MRSI voxel as the
maximum value in the HDO peak. To standardize the SNR
computation across all subjects, the subject signal was defined
as the mean of the voxel-wise signal in the top 15% of voxels
in that subject. This signal was divided by the noise estimated
from the standard deviation of the spectral region covering the
first 25% of the number of spectral points (e.g. first 64 spectral points for the 256 point acquisition). SNR efficiency is
then the SNR divided by the square root of the total sequence
time. The baseline for comparison in each experiment follows
acquisition details described Section 2.6, with modifications
in each experiment as described below. All SNR comparison
study results are summarized in Table 2 .

Pulse sequence modifications were tested in both healthy 65
human subjects (n=4) as well as the phantom. We sought to 66
improve SNR by optimizing repetition time, flip angle and k- 67
space sampling in our 3D MRSI sequence while maintaining 68
the 10x10x10 cm spatial array and 2.4 cm isotropic resolution, 69
70
with a sampling rate of 1 kHz.
For phantom studies, we compared TR values of 155, 200, 71
283, and 350 ms with Ernst angle RF excitation for natural 72
abundance deuterium (flip angles of 52, 56, 64 and 69 degrees 73
respectively) to our baseline of 350 ms TR, 90 degree flip 74
angle acquisition. All other acquisition parameters were held 75
constant except for the number of acquisition points, which 76
was reduced from 256 to 128 for the 155 and 200 ms TR acqui- 77
sitions. We then conducted SNR studies on a subset of three 78
acquisitions in 4 healthy controls, with each subject scanned 79
with 4 averages of each MRSI sequence. Improvements in 80
SNR efficiency in the human studies were evaluated with the 81
Wilcoxon signed-rank test. These parameters are summarized 82
83
in Table 2 .
All sequence modifications improved SNR efficiency 84
from our baseline acquisition sequence in both phantom and 85
human studies (Table 2 ). We find that SNR efficiency is 86
maximized in phantom studies by shortening TR to either 87
155 or 200 ms. In human studies, both the TR of 283 and 88
155 ms acquisitions improved SNR efficiency over base- 89
line (p<0.005), however there was no significant difference 90
between the two modified sequences in human subjects. As 91
such we intend to use the sequence with a TR of 283 ms mov- 92
ing forward, as decreasing as the number of acquisition points 93
to accommodate the shorter TR sequences reduces spectral 94
95
resolution without any measurable gain in SNR efficiency.

Reducing noise conducted by the gradient drivers was essential for enabling DMI studies on our GE Signa 3T PET/MR
scanner. We note that not all scanners will have this issue,
so we recommend researchers classify the noise characteristics of their system before making any modifications to the
gradient filters.
Prior human-subject DMI studies at higher field strengths
have used multi-coil arrays 37,44,45 . While useful for decreasing imaging times via parallel imaging techniques, at lower
frequencies, coil and front-end electronics noise increasingly
dominate over that from the body, resulting in poor phasedarray SNR performance for low-field (≤ 1T) scanners 52,53,54 .
With a gyromagnetic ratio of 6.53 MHz/T, the 2 H resonant
frequency is 19.59 Mhz at 3T; equivalent to a 0.46T 1 H scanner. Putting aside the advantages of uniform excitation and
reception B1 fields, our calculations suggest 3T DMI SNR
would be maximized via a single-channel volume 2 H coil
design. Crucially for research purposes, the 2 H birdcage coil

Following the oral ingestion of deuterated glucose, we used a
modified gradient filter, home-built 2 H volume RF coil, and
spherical k-space sampling in a 3D CSI acquisition to obtain
high quality whole-brain metabolic images of 2 H-labeled
water and glucose metabolic products. We explored the ability to quantify local glucose metabolism in correspondence
to anatomical landmarks across patients with varying CNS
lesions, and see evidence of the Warburg effect in one patient
with the most active disease. We additionally reported optimized pulse sequence parameters for a 3D CSI DMI sequence
at 3T. Quantification remains limited by SNR and spatial
resolution, which in future work will be improved through
machine-learning based multi-modal image denoising.
